League of Nations - Timeline

Welcome to a showcase of 20 events from the League of Nations' surprisingly short lifetime, ranging from the year 1920 to 1939.


The First Meeting of the League of Nations

Image provided by Encyclopedia Britannica

The League held its first council meeting in Paris on 16 January 1920, six days after the Treaty of Versailles and the Covenant of the League of Nations came into force. On 1 November 1920, the headquarters of the League was moved from London to Geneva, where the first General Assembly was held on 15 November 1920.
The League of Nations officially came into existence on 10 January 1920. On 15 November 1920, 41 member states gathered in Geneva for the opening of the first session of the Assembly. This represented a large portion of existing states and corresponded to more than 70% of the world's population.

Where did they meet for the first session of Assembly?

Incorrect! They actually met in Geneva.

Italy/Corfu: Powerless, Overshadowed

Image provided by The Montreal Gazette or 'The Gazette'

Image provided by the Rochester Evening Journal and the Post Express

After WW1, the border between Greece and Albania was redrawn. Enrico Tellini, an Italian general, had the job of patrolling it. In August of 1923, Tellini and a few other Italian soldiers were murdered on the border. This led the current Italian leader, Benito Mussolini, to demand 50 million lira (which would today be around $1,750,000) from Greece as compensation, but Greece refused to pay (and also refused to accept some of Mussolini’s other terms). In response, Italy invaded and bombarded Corfu, and Greece appealed to the League of Nations for help. Italy would not accept the League’s demand for it to leave Corfu, nor would Britain and France agree on how to take further action against Italy, so the matter would eventually be passed to the Conference of Ambassadors, which would eventually rule that Italy would leave Corfu, but it also stated that Greece would have to pay. Italy, in the end, won.

What was the name of the Italian general that was assasinated, causing the Corfu incident?

Incorrect! Enrico Tellini was the one who was killed.

The Occupation of the Ruhr: Impatience at its worst

Image from the front page of the Chicago Daily Tribune (06/03/1923)

The Ruhr Crisis of 1923 was a pivotal event in post-World War I Europe when France and Belgium, frustrated by Germany's failure to meet reparations payments, occupied the industrial Ruhr region. Despite the League of Nations' mission to ensure peace and mediate conflicts, it proved ineffective in preventing or resolving this crisis. The League's inability to address economic and political grievances, coupled with its failure to provide a viable solution, underscored its limitations. This event marked a significant blow to the League's credibility, revealing its ineffectiveness in preventing larger geopolitical conflicts and contributing to the organization's diminishing influence in the years leading up to World War II.

Who chose to occupy the Ruhr?

Incorrect! France and Belgium were the ones who occupied the Ruhr region.

The Dawes Plan: A temporary solution

Image from the front page of the Chicago Daily Tribune (06/03/1923)

The Dawes Plan, implemented in 1924, was a crucial economic initiative aimed at addressing the reparations issue stemming from World War I. Named after American financier Charles G. Dawes, the plan sought to reorganize Germany's reparations payments to France and Belgium, facilitating their receipt and easing Germany's economic burden. The plan indirectly connected to the League of Nations as it was endorsed by the Allied powers and became a framework for stabilizing European economies. While the Dawes Plan helped temporarily alleviate economic tensions, its broader impact on European stability was limited, and it did not prevent the economic challenges that later contributed to the Great Depression. The League of Nations, despite its aspirations for collective security, faced ongoing economic and political complexities, and the Dawes Plan exemplifies the attempts to manage these challenges outside the League's direct purview.

Who endorsed the Plan? Who was it supposed to benefit?

Incorrect! The Allied powers were the ones who endorsed the Plan, and it was supposed to benefit Germany.

The Locarno Treaties: An attempted step towards the future

Image provided by the United States Library of Congress

Image provided by the United States Library of Congress

Signed in Locarno, Switzerland, in 1925, the Locarno Treaties aimed to stabilize post-World War I Europe by securing territorial boundaries and fostering reconciliation among major powers. The Treaty of Mutual Guarantee, involving Germany, France, Belgium, Italy, and Britain, committed to peaceful dispute resolution and mutual defense. According to Eduqa Scott titled by Tutor2u, “these treaties recognized Germany's western borders and promoted arbitration between Germany and Poland, and Germany and Czechoslovakia.” While initially reducing tensions and fostering stability in the late 1920s, the treaties faced limitations, particularly in addressing broader security concerns and the League of Nations' shortcomings. Ultimately, they failed to prevent the outbreak of World War II amidst rising nationalism and economic challenges.

Incorrect! The League established the Lytton Commission, which ended up ruling that Japan had violated international law.

The Mukden Incident: No authority

Image provided by John D. Clare, made by an anonymous illustrator in 1933

Image provided by John D. Clare, illustrated by David Low in 1933

The Manchurian Crisis, also known as the Mukden Incident, unfolded in 1931 when the Japanese Kwantung Army orchestrated a staged explosion near a railway in Manchuria, providing a pretext to invade. The military swiftly took control, establishing the puppet state of Manchukuo. In response, the League of Nations dispatched the Lytton Commission, which found Japan in violation of international law. Despite the report recommending Manchuria's return to China, Japan rejected the League's conclusions and withdrew in 1933. This crisis underscored the League's incapacity to prevent aggression, dealt a blow to its authority, and set a precedent for subsequent acts of aggression. According to Kenneth Pletcher titled by Britannica “The League's failure to curb Japanese aggression revealed its limitations in enforcing collective security, contributing significantly to its decline and the onset of World War II.

Bolivia/Paraguay

Image provided by Encyclopedia Britannica

The Chaco War (Spanish: Guerra del Chaco, Guarani: Cháko Ñorairõ) was fought from 1932 to 1935 between Bolivia and Paraguay, over the control of the northern part of the Gran Chaco region (known in Spanish as Chaco Boreal) of South America, which was thought to be rich in oil.
On June 12, 1935, Paraguay and Bolivia signed the Peace Protocol after three years of war over Chaco territory. This historical event that occurred 86 years ago allows us to remember today the nearly 36,000 soldiers who lost their lives defending their country.

What were they fighting over?

Incorrect! They were fighting over a place that was rich in oil.

The World Disarmament Conference: The opposite effect

Image provided by Encyclopedia Britannica & the Smith Archive/Alamy

Image provided by the League of Nations Archives, Library of the United Nations Office at Geneva

After the Locarno Pact of 1925, the League of Nations set up a preparatory commission to prepare for a Disarmament Conference to be held in Geneva. During the meetings, the idea of German equality surged, and the German chancellor proposed their rearmament as a solution, and later his death and the surge of the Nazi party only further boosted this idea. When the Conference for the Reduction and Limitation of Armaments actually took place in February of 1932, the question of German equality dominated all others and Germany would be unsatisfied when the conference would be paused in July, so they chose to withdraw from negotiations. Other powerful countries sparked a promise to give Germany equality, and the conference resumed soon after in February of 1933, but, although there were other, more powerful countries against German rearmament, fear and mistrust caused by Adolf Hitler’s rise to power made it harder for the conference to progress, and Italy ended up siding with Germany, creating a pact that would, according to Walter A. McDougall from Encyclopedia Britannica, “grant Germany equality, revise the peace treaties, and establish a four-power directorate to resolve international disputes.” The pact was eventually signed and Germany began arming shortly after, and the conference was soon abandoned in 1934, shutting down further prospects of disarmament.

What was the Geneva Conference's official name?

Incorrect! It was the Conference for the Reduction and Limitation of Armaments.

Japan, Italy, and Germany quit the League of Nations: A temporary solution

Image provided by The Montreal Gazette or 'The Gazette'

Image provided by the Rochester Evening Journal and the Post Express

Image provided by the Rochester Evening Journal and the Post Express

In the 1930s, the world economic depression encouraged nations to be more aggressive towards each other. Fascist dictatorships took power in Germany, Italy and Japan, which were intent on empire-building and these countries defied the League.
After some notable successes and some early failures in the 1920s, the League ultimately proved incapable of preventing aggression by the Axis powers in the 1930s. The credibility of the organisation was weakened by the fact that the United States never joined, and Japan, Germany and Italy quit in 1933–1934.

What type of government was on the rise in the countries like Germany, Italy, and Japan (at the time)?

Incorrect! All these countries established dictatorships at the time.

The Italo-Ethiopian War: Nothing they could do

Image provided by John D. Clare and GCSE History

Image provided by John D. Clare and IGCSE History

Previously, in the 1980s, Italy had attempted to conquer Abyssinia (now Ethiopia) but failed, and Abyssinia was, in 1934, now one of the only countries left in Africa that wasn’t under European rule. However, a border dispute between Abynissia and Italian Somaliland (an Italian colony in Africa) gave the current Italian dictator, Benito Mussolini, an excuse to get involved. After refusing multiple arbitration (when an entity oversees and regulates a dispute, like how an arbiter regulates a football match) offers, Mussolini decided to invade Abyssinia on October 3, 1935. The Italian invaders pushed back the insufficiently-armed Ethiopian troops and would soon conquer the capital less than half a year later. The League of Nations morally condemned Italy’s actions, but Mussolini ignored it and pushed forward. The League later voted to impose economic sanctions on them, but they were ineffective because the major powers were not interested in opposing Italy. According to the Encyclopedia Britannica, the conflict “contributed to international tensions between the fascist states (like Italy) and the Western democracies, and served as a rallying point, especially after World War II, for developing African nationalist movements.” Finally, as said by the historian AP Adamthwaite, "the Abyssinian crisis delivered a death blow to the League."

What measure did the League of Nations attempt to use against Italy? (there are multiple answers)

Incorrect! The League used economic sanctions and moral condemnation.

The Remilitarization of the Rhineland: Treaty no more

Image provided by Punch Limited

Image provided by the British National Archives

Ever since the Treaty of Versailles was signed, the Rhineland had been taken away from Germany and was demilitarized as stated in the treaty’s terms. After 1933, however, other nations began to worry about their safety with Germany after Adolf Hitler became its chancellor, and he’d frequently voiced that he would stop at nothing to abolish the Treaty of Versailles. In 1935, France signed a treaty of friendship with the USSR, and Germany claimed it was hostile against them and Hitler used this to send German troops into the Rhineland in March 1936, which violated the Treaty of Versailles’ and Locarno Treaties’ terms. Even after negotiations, Hitler refused to withdraw his troops and put pressure on the League of Nations to act, and it was unable to. France was in the middle of elections, and, as the British National Archives pointed out, "Britain thought that the Treaty was too harsh against Germany, leading them to do nothing." Hitler would eventually move on from the occupation of the Rhineland, soon seizing other territories until he reached Poland in September 1939.

What did Germany take advantage of in order to occupy the Rhineland?

Incorrect! Germany was provoked by a friendship treaty being signed between France and the USSR?
